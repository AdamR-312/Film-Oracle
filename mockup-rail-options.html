Reel One

The Lobby

The chandeliers dim by degrees, and the room remembers itself. Velvet rope, brass guides, and the soft conspiracy of murmurs in the foyer. Somewhere overhead, the projection booth wakes from its cool sleep, the carbon arcs warming gold.

Ushers move between rows with small flashlights cupped in their palms. Each beam is the length of an arm — discreet, polite, never lingering. The carpet absorbs every footfall. The room is louder closed than open.

A girl shakes a paper bag of sweets. A father sets down two glasses of dark fizz on a low table. The marquee outside burns the rain to gold.

Reel Two

The Auditorium

The seat folds underneath you with a small mechanical sigh. Centre stalls, two-thirds back. The screen waits — a great pale rectangle held in by gold curtains, both ropes drawn tight by some unseen stagehand. Above, the ornament of the ceiling: a constellation of small dim bulbs in plaster vines.

A child in the row ahead asks if the film has started. Not yet. The lights dim again, halfway this time, and then a slow stretch toward black. Voices fall to whispers. The curtains begin to part.

This is the long held breath cinema is built around. Everyone has agreed, without saying so, to be still for the next two hours.

Reel Three

The Booth

Up the iron stairs. Past a door with a small red bulb above it. Inside: two projectors, brass and black, paired like draught horses. The reels spin, eight inches wide, threaded through a labyrinth of rollers and into the gate.

The projectionist sits on a stool, listens for the cue marks at the corner of the frame. A small dot, then another, twelve seconds later. He throws the lever. The second projector takes over without seam. No one downstairs notices.

Heat radiates from the lamphouse. The smell is of warm celluloid and oil. The film moves at twenty-four pictures a second, each held for a fraction of a fraction in the gate, then pulled away. The pulldown is what makes cinema cinema.

Reel Four

The Marquee

The letters are pegged into the marquee one by one with a long brass pole, by a man who has done this for thirty-two years. He calls the order in his head — title, then year, then showtimes. The wind moves the letters slightly in their slots.

Below, the box office glows like a lantern. A queue forms in the rain. Each ticket is the colour of bone, with a small gold star in the corner.

At the corner of every marquee: the small framed posters. Coming soon. Some of them are good. Some of them will be remembered.

Reel Five

The Reel

And then the credits, and the slow rise of the houselights, and the shuffle of coats and bags. A few people stay to read every name, out of love or politeness or because that's how their parents taught them.

The projectionist rewinds. The reel is heavy. He cradles it like a child as he carries it back to the cabinet. Tomorrow, another film. Or maybe the same one again.

Outside: the marquee, still bright. The rain has stopped. The pavement gleams.
